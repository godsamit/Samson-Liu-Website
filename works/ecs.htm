ECS

OUTDOOR LEARNING IN THE CITY

Project Date: 2017 Spring Studio | Individual

PROJECT AND SITE

Pittburgh's famous Environmental Charter School (ECS) is seeking to build a new campus for its middle school division. ECS is famous for incorporating environmental education and outdoor experiences into its core curriculum. The current school is located near Frick Park, which is the largest municipal park in Pittsburgh. Students are often encouraged to go into the forest for sports, activities, and observations.

The chosen site for the expansion is at Larimer, an up-and-coming neighborhood among the busy streets of the city. What's more, the square footage of the lot is also quite small. These conditions pose challenges for incorporating ECS's core value into the building design. On the plus side, there's no obstruction on the south of the site, ensuring plenty of sunlight.

DESIGN PROCESS

The primary goal of the design is to to ensure abundant access to nature and outdoor activities. Therefore, early on in the design process, I decided on the feature of staggered terraces, where each classroom would have their own outdoor garden, as seen in B.I.G's mountain. However, unlike the mountain, where each terrace is private, in ECS, the terraces are supposed to be designated for public outdoor activities. For this reason, I made sure that the terraces connect to each other, across all 3 levels. The hope is to encourage outdoor movements, and to leave as much flexibility as possible for assorted outdoor activities.

Except for the access to nature, ECS is also famous for its classroom structures for fostering collaboration and critical thinking. In an ECS classroom, students are often split into groups to conduct activities, then converge at the end of the class to discover their findings. Trying to foster such classes, I played around with the shape the classroom to better accommodate for both the "split" and "converge" scenarios. As demonstrated, this shape change directly changed the structural grid to a diamond grid.

With a basic parti, I started laying out the design through several iterations on trace paper, both in plan and section. I carefully lined up the structural grid, and constantly measured everything to estimate the scale of spaces. In the meantime, I constructed models to help explore and study the space and construction.

During these studies, I realized that I had to shift certain columns to either meet the program requirements, or to fit egress towers, facilities and services. In the process, the already shifted diamond grid got even more irregular. To better accommodate for this irregular grid, I chose the 2-way concrete slab as the structural system, hoping that it would save time and energy during the construction phase. Lastly, as a quick comparative study, I did some preliminary calculations for column and beam/floor sizes. The result shows that the floor would be 2-6" thinner than a composite beam construction, saving even more material while leaving a bigger headroom.

I was unsure about the orientation of the gardens. It could be equally tempting to have the gardens face the opposite way, i.e. the crossroads and East Liberty, to allow for more publicity and openness.
