ECS

OUTDOOR LEARNING IN THE CITY

Project Date: 2017 Spring Studio | Individual

PROJECT AND SITE

Pittburgh's famous Environmental Charter School (ECS) is seeking to build a new campus for its middle school division. ECS is famous for incorporating environmental education and outdoor experiences into its core curriculum. The current school is located near Frick Park, which is the largest municipal park in Pittsburgh. Students are often encouraged to go into the forest for sports, activities, and observations.

The chosen site for the expansion is at Larimer, an up-and-coming neighborhood among the busy streets of the city. What's more, the square footage of the lot is also quite small. These conditions pose challenges for incorporating ECS's core value into the building design. But on the plus side, there's no obstruction on the south of the site, ensuring students' access to plenty of natural daylight.

DESIGN PROCESS

The primary goal of the design is to to ensure abundant access to nature and outdoor activities. Therefore, early on in the design process, I decided on the feature of staggered terraces, where each classroom would have their own outdoor garden, as seen in B.I.G's mountain. However, unlike the mountain, where each terrace is private, in ECS, the terraces are supposed to be designated for public outdoor activities. For this reason, I made sure that the terraces connect to each other, across all 3 levels. The hope is to encourage outdoor movements, and to leave as much flexibility as possible for assorted outdoor activities. Lastly, due to the high local crime rates, I raised the gardens above the street level. This also helps to reduce the influence from the noises and emmission off of the quite busy Frankstown Avenue.

Other than the access to nature, ECS is also famous for its classroom structures for fostering collaboration and critical thinking. In an ECS classroom, students are often split into groups to conduct activities, then converge at the end of the class to discover their findings. Trying to foster such classes, I played around with the shape the classroom to better accommodate for both the "split" and "converge" scenarios. As demonstrated, this shape change directly changed the structural grid to a diamond grid.

With a basic parti, I started laying out the design through several iterations on trace paper, both in plan and section. I carefully lined up the structural grid, and constantly measured everything to estimate the scale of spaces. In the meantime, I constructed models to help explore and study the space and construction.

During these studies, I realized that I had to shift some columns to either meet the program requirements, or fit egress towers, facilities and services. In the process, the already shifted diamond grid got even more irregular. To better accommodate for this irregular grid, I chose the 2-way concrete slab as the structural system, hoping that it would save time and energy during the construction phase. Lastly, as a quick comparative study, I did some preliminary calculations for column and beam/floor sizes. The result shows that the floor would be 2-6" thinner than a composite beam construction, leaving a bigger headroom for occupants. If the construction adopts bubble slabs, it would save even more material.

With every program set in place, I generated a digital model and ran simulations. I used simulations to help my design process in mainly 2 aspects:

Wind simulation helped me decide the final orientation of the gardens. At one point in the design process, I considered flipping the design along its longitude: it's very tempting to have the design open up towards the crossroads and East Liberty, to allow for more publicity and openness. Though the radiation study turned out inconclusive, the wind simulation revealed that the current configuration helps blocking the harsh winter wind of pittsburgh off of the outdoor gardens. Therefore, I decided to keep the current orientation, believing that it will encourage students to go outside even during the winter. Glare analysis helped me nailed down using vertical, operatable louvers as the daylighting system. The south-facing classrooms have great access to southern sunlight, but this also brings undesirable glare issues. I was testing between a few different choices, considering both aesthetics and effectiveness. Though I love the look of vertical louvers, they are not the most ideal in handling N-S glares. However, if they are adjustable, they can be rather effective. In the design, the louvers would normally have their motors off and rotate with wind, but in case of strong glare, they can be adjusted to be parallel to the windows. Rolling curtains are also installed as one extra measure. Lastly, considering pittsburgh's higher latitude and the months of occupancy, I decided that the low-hanging sun can usually reach deep into the classroom. However, I installed light shelves on the first floor, to help the natural daylight reach deeper into the sport court.

FINAL DESIGN

During the integrated design workshop held by the studio, I got to consult structure and HVAC engineers, contractors, contractors and the client to look for feedback and advice on my design.

The final design integrated building systems in the following aspects:

HVAC: All the classrooms and office areas are ventilated by fan-coil unit ventilators. A separate packaged rooftop unit and duct system is adopted for the public spaces and passage ways. Only exhaust in the bathroom. Rainwater collection: A rainwater collection is integrated and calculated. Solar Panels: Solar panels' capacities are calculated.
