Smoke and Mirror

DECEIT, ILLUSIONS, AND LOW RELIEF PLASTER

Project Date: 2018 Spring | Collaborative

STUDIO IN THE MUSEUM

Low Relief is an experimental studio that also serves as a museum exhibit. The studio researches the rich tradition of "deceit" in architecture and seeks to combine it with modern hybrid media. The studio focuses on historical research, hands-on material play and new media exploration. The studio is located within Carnegie Museum of Art, and everything we did was on display during the entire semester. Therefore, we were constantly challeged to convey our progress through design towards visitors.

The first phase of the studio included visits to plaster shops in New York City and investigations into mixed reality techniques on architecture. Combining both experiences paved way for the later projects. We made small animations to project on our boards as well.

FAKE COLUMN

As a transitional project, our team were interested in using mirror and glass to create illusions. Inspired by the anamorphic drawings, we drew a connection between the cylindrical mirror and the most classic architectural element: a column. We then were inspired to explore the possibility of making a "fake column". Started with straight grooves to create a classic column shaft, we experimented to morph the grooves and see what comes out on the "column".

Then we naturally decided to connect the 3 fans together to a circle, to create a pattern where the morphing gets increasingly drastic. Due to time and material constraints, we only casted parts of the base in plaster, and chose a mixture of printing and projection to complete the circle.

For the column capital, we decided to quickly experiment another mirror-related illusion: mirror hologram. For the animation, we decided to demonstrate an animation hinting at the fabrication of a column base.

CORNER STUDY

In the final project, our team was interested in combining plaster textures and mirrors to imply non-physical spaces. We chose to start with a simple corner geometry, then try to see how much we could push the extents of space within the corner.

Throuout the project, texture and illusion testing were going in parallel. In the final installation, different textures made by and and through robotic process highlights the versatility of plaster, and in combination with the lights and mirror, further the potentials of a volume. Through the formation of multiple illusions in relation to the architectural corner, a viewer is left to continuously wonder about the reality of what they are seeing and of the object's finite nature.

The final full-scale product was exhibited in the Hall of Architecture of the Carnegie Museum of Art, as part of a public event celebrating the efforts of the studio and the work of the museum's Hall of Architecture.
