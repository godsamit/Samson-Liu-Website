FRESHWATER GALLERY,
ON WATER

Project Date: 2017 Fall Studio | Individual

PROJECT AND SITE

Pittburgh's Brunot Island has been "deserted" for almost 30 years. Various plans have been proposed to re-utilize the real estate. This studio proposes to build a freshwater gallery on the island, to introduce people to the pollutions and countermeasures in the Ohio River, thus to attract visitors and educate local people.

The studio was very open in terms of site choices: All of the island south to the Brunot Bridge was fair game. I moved through different schemes very quickly to see what makes sense. In the end, I decided that I wanted to connect the building to the bridge.

DESIGN PROCESS

Putting the gallery close to the bridge benefited my design in the following ways:

On this deserted island, the Brunot Bridge is one of the few prominent historical structures. It enriches the immediate context, grounds my proposal and ties it to the history of the island; As a "water pavilion", putting it close and above water makes the experience more immersive, allowing for more ways of interactions with the river; From an access standpoint, it makes the building more accessible to the public, especially bikers, by connecting the existing footbridges to the bike tracks nearby. Otherwise, the island is very much secluded; Putting the proposal out onto the river increases the visibility of the proposal to the public, which is crucial in this project. But since it remains in the non-barge area, it doesn't interrupt with the busy barge traffic on the Ohio River.
I moved through process models to explore the structural possibility to have a building hanging under a bridge. I worked the idea of "frames" into the design: A series of frames are hung from the bridge, lined up with the bridge's truss. These frames serve as the main structure for the building. Each frame can be relatively independent and they can have different shapes, Therefore, it allowed for oppotunities to create various rises and gaps on the floor and roof level, adding to the experience. Then, I worked on making the frames read lighter to create a bigger gallery space.

FINAL DESIGN

The final design explored various experiences this unique structure can offer to visitors.

Inside the building, there are various floor-level changes. Along with the programming decisions, this design aims to create a narratively meaningful spatial sequence for visitors. For example, entering from the island, visitors first descend and walk around the pier, appreciating the historical bridge and its history with the island. Then they arrive at the "water monitoring lab", appreciating the current effort on water preservation on Ohio river. At last, the visitors ascend to the final gallery. The final gallery features practices around the world and future scenarios. As the floor becomes suddenly open, and the views widened, the visitors will gain a broader understanding and appreciation towards water preservation.

From the platforms along the riverside to the glass floor beside the gallery, the building incorporates a variety of features that encourages visitors to interact with the river. The design provides both subtle teases, like the gaps between the floors, and full-on open views, where the entire river becomes the backdrop for the outdoor theater.
